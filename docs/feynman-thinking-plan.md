# Learn to Think Like Richard Feynman

## A Daily Practice Plan

**Goal ID:** `379dd881` (job-star, idle-opportunistic, personal)
**Format:** ~15–25 minutes per day. Each day is one small, concrete exercise.
**Philosophy of this plan:** You cannot *study* your way into thinking like Feynman. You can only *practice* it. This plan is built around doing, not reading. Reading is assigned only when it directly fuels an exercise.

---

## What "Thinking Like Feynman" Actually Means

Before the plan, here's the decomposition. Feynman's thinking has six identifiable engines:

| # | Engine | One-line description | Key Feynman quote |
|---|--------|---------------------|-------------------|
| 1 | **The Feynman Technique** | Explain it simply → find gaps → go back → simplify | "If you can't explain it to a freshman, you don't understand it." |
| 2 | **First-Principles Reasoning** | Strip away analogy and convention. Reason from the most basic truths upward. | "What I cannot create, I do not understand." |
| 3 | **Radical Simplicity / Anti-Jargon** | Plain language or you don't understand it. Complexity is a red flag, not a sign of sophistication. | "You can recognize truth by its beauty and simplicity." |
| 4 | **Curiosity-Driven Inquiry** | Follow your actual interest, not the syllabus. Obsession is the engine. | "The prize is the pleasure of finding the thing out." |
| 5 | **Visual & Physical Intuition** | Think in pictures, mechanisms, and motion. Diagrams before equations. | "I can't understand anything in general unless I'm playing with a specific example." |
| 6 | **Radical Honesty / Comfort with Not Knowing** | "I don't know" is the beginning, not the end. Never fool yourself. | "The first principle is that you must not fool yourself — and you are the easiest person to fool." |

These six engines interlock. The plan trains each one, then combines them.

## Phase 1: The Feynman Technique (Weeks 1–2)

> **Goal:** Make the Feynman Technique a reflex. You should be able to pull it out on demand for anything.

### The Core Loop (memorize this)

```
1. Pick a concept
2. Explain it as if to a smart 12-year-old (write it down)
3. Identify where you got stuck, hand-waved, or used jargon
4. Go back to the source material, learn the gaps
5. Rewrite the explanation, even simpler
6. Use an analogy from daily life
```

---

### Week 1 — The Basic Loop

**Day 1: Explain something you think you know.**
Pick a concept you use regularly but have never deeply explained — e.g., "how a zipper works," "what a database index is," "why the sky is blue." Write a 1-page explanation for a 12-year-old. No jargon. No hand-waving. When you hit a wall, mark it with `[GAP]` and keep going.

**Day 2: Fill the gaps.**
Take yesterday's explanation. Look only at the `[GAP]` sections. Go learn *just enough* to fill each gap. Rewrite those sections. Do not rewrite the whole thing — only the broken parts. This is the feedback loop.

**Day 3: Add an analogy.**
Take Day 1's concept. Create an analogy from a completely different domain (cooking, sports, carpentry, traffic). Write it out. Then ask: *where does the analogy break down?* Write down the breakdown. Feynman loved analogies but never trusted them completely — he always knew where they stopped working.

**Day 4: New concept, full loop.**
Pick a new concept — something slightly harder (e.g., "how TCP handshakes work," "what inflation is," "how vaccines train the immune system"). Run the full loop: explain → gap → learn → simplify → analogy. Time yourself: 25 minutes, no more.

**Day 5: Explain it out loud.**
Take any concept from this week. Explain it *out loud* to an imaginary person (or a rubber duck, or your phone's voice recorder). Talking forces you to find the sequence — you can't skip ahead like you can in writing. Notice where you stumble. Those are your gaps.

**Day 6: The "so what?" test.**
Take your best explanation from the week. Read it and ask: "So what? Why does this matter?" If your explanation doesn't convey *why anyone should care*, rewrite the opening. Feynman always started with the significance, not the definition.

**Day 7: Rest / free exploration.**
No assigned exercise. Follow whatever catches your curiosity today. If something hooks you, spend 10 minutes going down the rabbit hole. The goal is to keep the engine of curiosity warm.

---

### Week 2 — Pressure-Testing the Technique

**Day 8: Explain something you DON'T fully understand.**
Pick something genuinely hard that you've been avoiding — a concept that's been fuzzy for years. Run the loop. The point isn't to master it today; it's to discover the exact shape of your ignorance. Write down: "I thought I understood X, but actually I don't understand Y."

**Day 9: The jargon hunt.**
Take any article, documentation page, or textbook chapter you read today. Highlight every piece of jargon or "impressive-sounding" phrase. For each one, write the plain-language equivalent. If you can't write one, mark `[FAKE UNDERSTANDING]`. Feynman's move: if a word doesn't add meaning, it's hiding a gap.

**Day 10: Explain it in 3 sentences.**
Take the concept from Day 8. Compress your explanation to exactly 3 sentences. No more. This forces you to find the *structure* of the idea — what's essential, what's decoration. If you can't do 3 sentences, you don't know what matters yet.

**Day 11: The child's "why" chain.**
Explain a concept, then imagine a child asking "why?" after each statement. Follow the chain at least 5 levels deep. You'll hit "I don't know" fast — that's the point. Map the chain and mark where you ran out.

**Day 12: Teach it backward.**
Take a concept you understand. Explain it starting from the *end* (the result, the output, the effect) and work backward to the cause. This breaks your memorized sequence and forces genuine understanding. Feynman could explain things from any direction because he understood the structure, not just the sequence.

**Day 13: The Feynman Technique on a FEELING or OPINION.**
Not just facts. Pick something you believe or feel strongly about (a political view, a design preference, a life decision). Apply the loop: explain *why* you believe it to a 12-year-old. Find the gaps. You may discover your reasoning is weaker than you thought. This is Feynman's intellectual honesty applied to yourself.

**Day 14: Rest / reflect.**
Write 1 paragraph: "What did I learn about my own thinking these two weeks?" Be honest. Where did you fake understanding? Where did you surprise yourself?

## Phase 2: First-Principles Reasoning (Weeks 3–4)

> **Goal:** Learn to strip away convention and analogy, and reason from bedrock.

### The Core Move

```
1. Identify the thing you want to understand
2. List every assumption behind it (write them ALL down)
3. For each assumption, ask: "Is this a fundamental truth, or is it convention/analogy?"
4. Keep only the fundamental truths
5. Build the reasoning back up from those truths alone
```

---

### Week 3 — Identifying Assumptions

**Day 15: The assumption inventory.**
Pick something ordinary: "Why do we drive on the right side of the road?" or "Why do work weeks have 5 days?" List 10 assumptions behind it. Don't filter yet — just list. The skill is *seeing* assumptions, which are usually invisible.

**Day 16: Separate truth from convention.**
Take Day 15's list. For each assumption, label it: `TRUTH` (physics/logic demands it) or `CONVENTION` (we just do it this way). Be rigorous. "We drive on the right because most people are right-handed" — is that a truth or a convention?

**Day 17: First-principles a physical object.**
Pick an object near you (a coffee mug, a keyboard, a light bulb). Ask: "What is this, *fundamentally*?" Strip away everything that's design choice. What are the physical necessities? What are the constraints imposed by physics? What's left if you remove all human convention?

**Day 18: First-principles a SYSTEM you work with.**
Pick something from your daily work: "What is a REST API, fundamentally?" or "What is a database, fundamentally?" List assumptions, strip conventions, build from the core. What is the irreducible minimum? What's decoration?

**Day 19: The "why does this exist?" drill.**
Pick a tool, process, or institution. Ask "Why does this exist?" Keep asking "why" until you hit either a fundamental truth or a "because someone decided." Most things exist because of accumulated decisions, not necessity. Seeing this is the first-principles skill.

**Day 20: First-principles a PROBLEM you're stuck on.**
Take a real problem you're facing (technical or personal). Write down every assumption you're making about the problem. Now ask: "If I remove this assumption, does the problem still exist?" Most stuck-ness comes from inheriting constraints you didn't choose.

**Day 21: Rest / free exploration.**

---

### Week 4 — Building From Scratch

**Day 22: Rebuild from zero.**
Take a concept from Phase 1. Now rebuild it from first principles. If you had never seen this concept before, and only knew the fundamental truths, would you arrive at the same thing? Write the derivation. Compare to the "standard" explanation. Where do they differ?

**Day 23: The "could it be different?" test.**
Take a system or concept. Ask: "Could this be fundamentally different and still work?" If yes, why is it the way it is? (Convention? Historical accident?) If no, what physical/logical constraint forces it? This separates necessity from habit.

**Day 24: First-principles a disagreement.**
Take something you disagree with someone about. Write their position. Now steelman it from first principles — what would have to be true for them to be right? You don't have to agree, but you must find the strongest version. Feynman respected arguments that were built well, even if he disagreed with the conclusion.

**Day 25: Derive something you "just know."**
Pick something you know by rote (a formula, a rule of thumb, a best practice). Derive it from first principles. Why does it work? What assumptions make it valid? When would it break? If you can't derive it, you don't *know* it — you *memorized* it.

**Day 26: The constraint-removal game.**
Take a problem. List its constraints. Remove one constraint at random. Solve the problem without it. Then ask: "Was that constraint real, or was I just assuming it?" Feynman solved problems by questioning the constraints everyone else accepted.

**Day 27: Combine Phase 1 + Phase 2.**
Take a concept. First, apply first-principles to find the core. Then, apply the Feynman Technique to explain that core simply. This is the combined move: *simple explanations built on true foundations*.

**Day 28: Rest / reflect.**
Write 1 paragraph: "What assumptions have I been carrying that I didn't realize were assumptions?"

## Phase 3: Curiosity & Visual Thinking (Weeks 5–6)

> **Goal:** Train the curiosity engine and the visual/spatial intuition that Feynman relied on.

---

### Week 5 — Curiosity-Driven Inquiry

**Day 29: The "I wonder why..." journal.**
Start a curiosity journal. Today, write down 5 things you noticed and wondered about. Small things: "Why does my dog turn in circles before lying down?" "Why does coffee taste different when it cools?" "Why do I type faster when I'm not thinking about typing?" Don't answer them yet. Just collect.

**Day 30: Follow ONE rabbit hole.**
Pick the most interesting question from Day 29. Spend 20 minutes following it. Go wherever it leads. The only rule: you must follow the *most interesting* next question, not the most *practical* one. Feynman's best work came from following curiosity, not the syllabus.

**Day 31: The "obvious" question.**
Find something you encounter daily that you've never questioned. Ask the obvious question about it. "Why are manhole covers round?" (Feynman loved this category.) Spend 15 minutes actually answering it. The skill is *noticing the invisible familiar*.

**Day 32: Cross-domain curiosity.**
Read about something completely outside your field. A topic you know nothing about. Find one connection to something you DO know. Write it down. Feynman's genius was partly his ability to connect physics to biology, to art, to everyday phenomena.

**Day 33: The "what if" game.**
Take a physical or social phenomenon. Ask "What if this were different?" "What if gravity were twice as strong?" "What if humans slept in 4-hour segments instead of 8?" Follow the implications as far as you can. This trains the *consequence-tracing* that Feynman did naturally.

**Day 34: Be wrong on purpose.**
Take something you understand. Make a deliberately wrong hypothesis about it. Then *steelman your wrong hypothesis* as well as you can. Then find the specific experiment or observation that disproves it. Feynman was unafraid of being wrong — he treated wrong ideas as interesting toys to play with until they broke.

**Day 35: Rest / free exploration.**

---

### Week 6 — Visual & Physical Intuition

**Day 36: Draw it.**
Pick a concept from Phase 1 or 2. Draw it. Not a chart or a diagram — a *picture*. A physical representation. Feynman thought in pictures. If you can't draw it, you're thinking in words, not in mechanisms. Stick figures are fine. Ugly is fine. But it must be visual.

**Day 37: The mechanism question.**
For any concept, stop asking "what" and start asking "how — mechanically?" "What happens when I press a key?" → "How, mechanically, does a keypress become a character on screen?" Trace the physical/electronic/information path. Feynman always wanted to know the *mechanism*, not just the description.

**Day 38: The motion question.**
Take a static concept (a data structure, a political system, a recipe). Ask: "What moves? What's in motion?" Visualize the dynamics. Feynman diagrams were revolutionary because they showed *motion and interaction*, not static relationships. Find the motion in your concept.

**Day 39: Build a physical model.**
Take a concept and represent it with physical objects on your desk (coins, rubber bands, cups). Act out the process. If you can't, you're missing something about the mechanism. This is Feynman's "play" — he literally played with physical objects to understand abstract ideas.

**Day 40: The extreme case.**
Take a concept and push it to an extreme. "What if there were 10 billion items instead of 10?" "What if the temperature were absolute zero?" "What if the speed of light were 10 mph?" The extreme case reveals the structure. Feynman loved edge cases — they expose what's essential.

**Day 41: The "draw it without labels" test.**
Draw a concept's mechanism. Now remove all text labels. Can someone who sees the drawing still understand what's happening? If not, your visual model depends on words, not on the picture itself. Feynman's diagrams worked even without text because they captured *process*.

**Day 42: Rest / reflect.**
Write 1 paragraph: "What did I discover by trying to visualize things I previously understood only in words?"

## Phase 4: Radical Honesty & Questioning Assumptions (Weeks 7–8)

> **Goal:** Internalize "you must not fool yourself" and learn to ask the questions that expose hidden assumptions.

---

### Week 7 — Intellectual Honesty

**Day 43: The "do I actually know this?" audit.**
List 10 things you "know." For each, ask honestly: "Do I know this, or have I just heard it repeated?" Rate each: `KNOW` (I can derive it / I've verified it), `HEARD` (someone told me and I believed it), `ASSUMED` (I've never even checked). Be brutal. The first step is seeing how much of your "knowledge" is `HEARD` or `ASSUMED`.

**Day 44: The source trace.**
Take one `HEARD` belief from Day 43. Trace it to its source. Who said it? What evidence did they have? Is it still true? You'll find many beliefs are inherited, not earned. Feynman refused to carry beliefs he hadn't earned.

**Day 45: The "what would change my mind?" test.**
Take a belief you hold. Write down: "What evidence, if it existed, would change my mind about this?" If you can't think of anything, you don't have a belief — you have a faith. Feynman always knew what would disprove his ideas. That's what made them scientific.

**Day 46: Catch yourself fooling yourself.**
Today, notice one moment where you *pretended to understand* something you didn't. It happens in conversations, in meetings, when reading. Notice the impulse to nod along. Write it down. The goal is to catch it in real-time. Feynman's superpower wasn't being smart — it was being *honest about what he didn't know*.

**Day 47: The "I don't know" practice.**
Today, every time you're about to explain something you're unsure about, say "I don't know" instead. Notice how it feels. Notice that nothing bad happens. Feynman said "I don't know" constantly. It's the most powerful phrase in science because it opens the door to learning.

**Day 48: The self-steelman.**
Take your strongest held opinion. Build the strongest possible argument *against* it. Not a strawman — the steelman. If you can't build the steelman, you don't understand the other side well enough to hold your opinion. Feynman could argue both sides of any debate because he understood the structure, not just the position.

**Day 49: Rest / reflect.**
Write 1 paragraph: "What did I learn about the difference between what I know and what I thought I knew?"

---

### Week 8 — The Questioning Move

**Day 50: The "what do we mean by" drill.**
Pick a commonly used word in your field (or in daily life): "intelligence," "efficient," "fair," "scalable," "consciousness." Ask: "What do we *actually mean* by this?" Write down 3 different definitions. Notice that people use the same word to mean different things, and most arguments are actually about definitions, not facts. This was Feynman's signature move — unmasking definitional confusion.

**Day 51: The hidden assumption finder.**
Take an argument or a "common sense" claim. Find the hidden assumption — the thing that must be true for the argument to work, but that nobody stated. Write it down. Feynman was legendary for finding the unstated assumption that made an entire argument collapse.

**Day 52: The "compared to what?" question.**
Take any claim ("X is fast," "X is expensive," "X is complicated"). Ask: "Compared to what?" Most claims are meaningless without a reference point. Feynman always demanded the frame of comparison. "Fast compared to walking? Compared to light? Compared to last year?"

**Day 53: The "who benefits?" question.**
Take a "best practice" or a convention. Ask: "Who benefits from this being the standard?" Not cynically — analytically. Many conventions exist because they benefit someone, not because they're optimal. Feynman was immune to authority-based arguments ("it's the standard because important people say so").

**Day 54: The "what would Feynman ask?" practice.**
Find a news article, a technical blog post, or a presentation. Read it. Then ask: "What would Feynman ask about this?" Write 3 questions. They'll usually be basic, almost rude in their simplicity. That's the point. The best questions are the ones that seem too simple to ask.

**Day 55: Combine all four phases.**
Take a real topic. (1) Identify the hidden assumptions. (2) Strip to first principles. (3) Explain the core simply. (4) Visualize the mechanism. (5) Be honest about what you still don't know. This is the full Feynman move.

**Day 56: Rest / reflect.**
Write 1 paragraph: "What questions have I been failing to ask?"

## Phase 5: Integration & Daily Habit (Weeks 9–10)

> **Goal:** Collapse all techniques into a sustainable daily habit that takes 10–15 minutes.

---

### Week 9 — The Daily Feynman Habit

**Day 57: The morning Feynman.**
Establish a daily habit: every morning, pick ONE thing you encountered yesterday that you didn't fully understand. Run the Feynman Technique loop on it. 10 minutes. No more. The point is consistency, not depth.

**Day 58: The evening audit.**
Establish a second habit: every evening, write one sentence answering "What did I pretend to understand today that I didn't?" This is the honesty engine, running daily.

**Day 59: The weekly first-principles.**
Establish a weekly habit: every week, pick ONE assumption you've been carrying and test it. Is it truth or convention? 15 minutes once a week.

**Day 60: The curiosity log.**
Keep the curiosity journal from Week 5 going. One entry per day minimum. "I noticed X and wondered about Y." Feed one entry per week into a rabbit-hole session.

**Day 61: The Feynman Technique on your OWN work.**
Take something you built or designed. Explain it as if to a 12-year-old. Find the gaps. You'll discover that you built things you don't fully understand — you assembled them from patterns. This is deeply humbling and deeply useful.

**Day 62: The Feynman Technique on a DECISION.**
Take a decision you made (technical architecture, a purchase, a life choice). Explain *why* you made it, simply. Find the hidden assumptions. Find where you fooled yourself. Feynman's thinking isn't just for science — it's for living.

**Day 63: Rest / reflect.**
Write 1 paragraph: "Which of these habits do I want to keep permanently?"

---

### Week 10 — Pressure-Testing the Habit

**Day 64: The Feynman gauntlet.**
Today, apply the Feynman Technique to THREE different things: one fact, one system, one opinion. 10 minutes each. This tests whether the technique has become a reflex or whether it still requires effort.

**Day 65: The Feynman Technique under pressure.**
Someone explains something to you today (in a meeting, in conversation, in a video). In real-time, silently apply the technique: "Can I restate what they just said in simple terms?" "Where did they lose me?" "What assumption are they making?" The goal is to do it live, not just in your journal.

**Day 66: Teach someone for real.**
Find a real person (friend, colleague, family member) and teach them something you've worked on in this plan. Use the simplest explanation you can. Watch their face for the moment they get it — or the moment they don't. Their confusion is your feedback. Adjust and try again.

**Day 67: The self-assessment.**
Write an honest self-assessment: For each of the six engines, rate yourself 1–5:
1. Feynman Technique (can I explain things simply?)
2. First-principles reasoning (can I find the bedrock?)
3. Radical simplicity (do I catch jargon-hiding?)
4. Curiosity-driven inquiry (do I follow my interest?)
5. Visual/physical intuition (do I think in pictures?)
6. Radical honesty (do I admit what I don't know?)

**Day 68: The 30-day commitment.**
Commit to the daily habit (Days 57–58) for 30 more days. Write the commitment down. The plan is done; the practice begins.

**Day 69: Read one Feynman source.**
Read one chapter of "Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman!" or watch one of his lectures. Don't study it — notice *how he thinks* in real time. You've now practiced enough to recognize his moves when you see them. This reading is the reward, not the input.

**Day 70: The final reflection.**
Write 1 page: "How has my thinking changed?" Be specific. Not "I think more deeply" — that's jargon. "I now ask 'compared to what?' when someone says 'fast.'" "I draw pictures before I write explanations." "I say 'I don't know' in meetings." Concrete changes or it didn't happen.

## The Permanent Daily Habit (Post-Plan)

After 70 days, the habit collapses to this:

```
MORNING (5 min):  Pick one thing from yesterday you didn't fully understand.
                  Explain it simply. Find one gap.

EVENING (2 min):  Write one sentence: "What did I pretend to understand today?"

WEEKLY (15 min):  Pick one assumption you've been carrying. Test it.

WEEKLY (15 min):  Follow one curiosity rabbit hole.

ANYTIME:          When you hear something impressive-sounding, ask:
                  "What does that actually mean? Can you explain it simply?"
```

That's it. Five small habits. Run forever.
